## Statistical Analysis

All statistical analyses were performed using R (version 4.4.2 or later). The analysis was designed to compare pertussis incidence rates across various vaccination program characteristics. Given that the underlying distribution of incidence data is often skewed and may not meet the assumptions of parametric tests, a non-parametric statistical approach was implemented for both estimation and hypothesis testing.

### 1. Estimation of Mean and 95% Confidence Intervals (CIs)

To provide a robust estimate of the central tendency and its uncertainty for each category, a non-parametric bootstrap procedure was used to calculate the mean incidence rate and its 95% confidence interval (CI).

For each group defined by a vaccination characteristic (e.g., "3 doses", "4 doses"), the following steps were taken:

Bootstrap Resampling: 1,000 bootstrap samples were generated by randomly drawing observations with replacement from the original group's data. Each bootstrap sample had the same size as the original group.
Distribution of the Mean: The mean was calculated for each of the 1,000 bootstrap samples, creating an empirical sampling distribution of the mean.
Confidence Interval Calculation: The 95% confidence interval was determined by taking the 2.5th and 97.5th percentiles of the bootstrap distribution of the means.
This method avoids assumptions about the normality of the data and provides a reliable estimate of the confidence interval for the population mean.

### 2. Pairwise Comparisons and Hypothesis Testing

To determine if the observed differences in mean incidence rates between pairs of categories were statistically significant, a permutation test was performed for all possible pairwise comparisons within each variable. The permutation test is a non-parametric method for testing the null hypothesis that two groups are drawn from the same underlying distribution.

The procedure for each pairwise comparison was as follows:

Observed Statistic: The actual difference in mean incidence rates between the two groups was calculated.
Permutation Procedure: The group labels of the data points were randomly shuffled (permuted) 5,000 times. In each permutation, the observations were randomly reassigned to the two groups (while preserving the original group sizes), and the difference in means was recalculated. This process generated an empirical null distribution of the mean difference, representing the range of differences expected if the null hypothesis were true.
P-Value Calculation: A two-sided p-value was computed as the proportion of permuted mean differences whose absolute value was greater than or equal to the absolute value of the originally observed mean difference.

### 3. Correction for Multiple Comparisons

Performing multiple pairwise tests inflates the probability of making a Type I error (a false positive). To control the family-wise error rate (FWER), the raw p-values from all pairwise permutation tests within a given variable were adjusted using the Holm-Bonferroni method. This is a step-down procedure that is uniformly more powerful than the standard Bonferroni correction while still providing strong control over the FWER.

The adjusted p-values were then used for interpretation and for the annotations on the figures.
